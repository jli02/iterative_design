Iterative Design: CUR8

designing a purchasing flow for a carbon removal brokerage

Team: Angie Osei-Ampadu, Sandy Martinez, Ashley Cai, Joyce Li

Introduction

CUR8 is a carbon removal brokerage on a mission to remove 1 billion tonnes of carbon a year. They "curate" custom carbon removal portfolios according to customers' needs and goals. The startup has started designing a portfolio simulation tool, where customers can input requirements and generate a portfolio of removal projects. They have asked us to continue on their design to create a purchasing flow so that users can buy the portfolios that they have generated.

Problem Statement: "CUR8's current manual carbon removal portfolio creation process limits user self-service and requires company assistance, constraining the user experience and slowing down customer acquisition."

We wanted to create an intuitive, simple purchasing flow with enough friction so that users can ensure that they are buying a portfolio that is best suited for their needs (as buying carbon removal projects can get very expensive!). We considered ways for customers to get feedback on their generated portfolios from stakeholders and how to make the process accessible to customers who might not have any prior experience with purchasing carbon removal projects.

Sketching

We each independently designed two solutions for the brief to get the widest possible spread of ideas for our mid-fidelity wireframe, resulting in 8 separate flows. While we did these independently, we realized that many aspects of our flows overlapped, which helped us decide what would be most intuitive for our first iteration of wireframes.


First Iteration Wireframing

In our sketches, we all had about the same pages: portfolio summary, cart and customer information, and a confirmation page. While we each broke different pages into multiple ones, we decided to begin with these three because they were the most essential based on our sketches and email with CUR8. We also added a collaborator page so that stakeholders can easily be added to the process, since the customers are usually teams working to determine their desired carbon tonnes removal and price.

Our email to Vitor (our primary point of contact in CUR8) and feedback from TAs was helpful in the group's understanding of what we were supposed to do: create a payment flow. In turn, our group tried to make the payment flow as natural and simple as possible by picking up where the CUR8 high fidelity mockup left off on the project summary, then adding other stakeholders who will be involved in the purchase decision, then to the information and payment page that ended with a confirmation page. We only included the four pages we deemed necessary so we don't confuse the customer or lose them before they make it to the purchase page. While we felt like we were missing certain elements, such as a page dedicated to the breakdown of each project, we decided to keep it simple and allow Vitor to guide us with his feedback for the next iteration. On design, we didn’t want to implement our style guide in our early iterations because we were still grasping customer needs and the scope of our work and didn’t want to misrepresent the brand.

Critique and Mid-project Check In

We then got feedback from our peers and CUR8 on our low fidelity wireframe.

Here's the Loom we presented to them.

Feedback from Vitor:
Vitor recommended adding a step between their portfolio simulator and our Step 5 (the first page of our wireframe) to give users more details and characteristics of specific projects that are included in their portfolio. He liked the step with adding stakeholders but encouraged us to be more creative with how we can allow stakeholders to leave feedback or comments. Likewise, he wanted us to send these stakeholders a more complete proposal of the portfolio, including a cost breakdown and projects included. Finally, he informed us that, realistically, users will not be able to purchase portfolios immediately but will instead have to send a purchase request to be approved by CUR8.

Feedback from Talie:
Talie advised us to revisit the Step 7 - Review Portfolio page and execute our vision of making it look like a receipt. Making the page look like a receipt was our original intention; however, hearing Talie’s feedback helped us realize that our vision was not coming across. We agreed on the left side of the screen being a portfolio summary and the right side containing a breakdown of each project.

Feedback from peers:
One of our peers said that “there are no back buttons on the pages. I think adding back buttons would add a lot of usability so that purchasers can go back and fix a small mistake without having to start the whole process over again.” This was something we missed and went back to implement. Another one of our peers said that her “one piece of feedback would be to make the "headers" of your lo-fi designs shorter.” For example, turn "send the details of your portfolio to stakeholders for sign off" into a shortened version like "email stakeholders. We implemented this by making the names of our pages and descriptions shorter, such as “Step 7 - Review Portfolio.”

Updated Wireframe

After our studio critique and mid-project check-in with CUR8, we modified the wireframe to reflect the feedback we received. CUR8 liked our initial project breakdown but asked us to include more specifics on the project with characteristics on the speed of drawdown, tech or nature-based, durability, location, and the supplier company. Step 5 below reflects those changes. Additionally, we added filters to allow the customer to filter projects in their portfolio by solution type so that they can more easily find the project they are looking for. CUR8 also included feedback about allowing the stakeholders invited to the portfolio to leave feedback. Steps 7 and 8 on “flow 2” reflect the invite the invited stakeholders from step 6 would see. Upon clicking the “Go To Portfolio'' button in Step 7, the invited stakeholder will see a detailed portfolio breakdown and will also have the opportunity to leave comments, feedback, and questions about the portfolio in a chat section on the left. Stakeholders will be able to see feedback other members of their team have left and CUR8 can view this communication/feedback to understand how their clients are thinking about the portfolio. Continuing with Flow 1, we reflected CUR8’s feedback about updating the purchasing step to be a purchase request because realistically clients won’t be able to purchase the portfolio without CUR8’s approval. This request page includes important information CUR8 needs to approve the purchase request such as the client’s name, email, address, and the reason for the portfolio. Lastly, we added the request confirmation and a button that leads clients back to step 5 so they can see their portfolio again.

Style Guide:


CUR8 provided us with a style guide that was in line with their Brand identity. We followed that style guide to ensure that the flow we designed fit in well with their current website and branding. We believe the startup chose the neutral and earth tones and images they have to reflect their climate-oriented business. Their background color, a very pale cream, contrasts well with the forest greens that highlight buttons, and appear in many images and color pages across their site. Additionally, we followed their design for cards which we used to display project information in the portfolio and textboxes which we use for sending invites to collaborators, getting feedback from clients, and filling out text fields for purchase requests.

We also chose to stick with their style guide because the brand kit CUR8 sent us was very consistent and reflected a strong brand identity. We don’t have suggestions for improving the style guide, because we felt it was already really well thought out and executed.


Interactive High Fidelity Prototype:

Check out our high fidelity Figma here!

For our high fidelity prototype, We chose colors that aligned with CUR8’s brand identity and had good contrast for accessibility, primarily cream, white, and green, and some deep purple, brown, and pinks. We also used images from the brand kit that had evoked CUR8’s climate-focused mission

Step 5 is the first page of our design and picks up from CUR8’s current flow which provides a summary of the user’s portfolio and total costs based on their inputs. This page provides a breakdown of projects within the portfolio. The portfolio overview including total is at the top and users can filter by project type. The page is scrollable so users can scroll down and see other projects. There is a previous button that navigates back to CUR8’s overview floor and a next button that takes you to the next page. The bar at the top shows the users their progress throughout the process.

Step 6: Upon clicking the next button on Step 5, a pop-up encouraging users to invite collaborators to view their portfolios and give feedback comes up. The background becomes green to contrast the white pop-up. Once users invite stakeholders they can see whether the stakeholder has viewed the portfolio and can also click a button that takes them directly to the stakeholder’s comments. We gave users the choice to skip this step in case they are the only CFO at the company or does not need other internal approval. The pop nature of this step reflects its optionality. Additionally, the pop-up appearing over the portfolio breakdown indiciates to users that the portfolio from Step 5 which is the background of the pop-up is what users are inviting stakeholders to view.

Step 7: After users either choose to add stakeholders or skip that step, they will be led to step 7, a review page. The idea of this page was to mimic a receipt that provided an itemized list and breakdown of the projects in the portfolio, highlighting the total cost, and important project characteristics such as the supplier company, project type, carbon tonnes removed, drawdown speed, and durability. On the right-hand side, the page includes a summary of the total portfolio showing the breakdown of project type included in the itemized portfolio. The listed design mimics a receipt which is intentional and meant to evoke the feeling of reviewing items in your shopping cart before purchasing.

Step 8 and 9: Once users review their portfolio, they are led to the purchase request page. If the user did not invite stakeholders in previous steps banner alert appears and the “open collaborators” button takes them back to Step 6 to invite stakeholders from their company to review the portfolio before the user submits a purchase request. If the user still wishes to not invite stakeholders, they can dismiss the notification. The second image shows the page users will see if 1) they already invite stakeholders in Step 6 and 2) if they click the “close notification” button. The top of this page includes important metrics such as the subtotal of the project and the carbon tonnes removed so users are reminded of their total and goal as they submit their purchase request. Users then fill out their information and submit the request. Upon submitting the request, users receive a confirmation message and can choose to return to their portfolio which will lead them back to Step 5 or exit the page.

Importantly, we made a second flow which shows that invited stakeholders in step 6 will see.

Once the user invites a stakeholder, the invited stakeholder will receive an email alert informing them that they have been invited to view their CUR8 portfolio solution where they can leave feedback and ask questions. The “Go To Portfolio” button leads users to the second page.

Below is the page invited stakeholders are directed to when they click the “Go to portfolio” button. This page includes a summary breakdown of the portfolio solutions on the left. This summary includes project type, speed of removal, durability, and geographic representation of the project. The middle of the page includes the projects in the portfolio and their characteristics. The right side of the page includes a comment/chat feature where invited stakeholders can leave questions and feedback for the portfolio. The chat design encourages clients to communicate with their team and allows them to see the feedback and questions their team members have. This will be helpful for CUR8 because they can see how clients understand the project and the different questions and feedback they have on the portfolio.

Additionally, in the main user flow in step 6, once the user has invited stakeholders, they can click the “see comments” button next to the invited stakeholder’s name once the stakeholder leaves a comment. This button leads them to the above page where stakeholders see the portfolio breakdown and leave comments and feedback.

Final Founder Feedback

We sent another Loom to CUR8 to get feedback on our high fidelity wireframe this time.

The feedback from CUR8 is included in our high fidelity mockup on Figma. You can reference our Figma (page:"high fid") for context on the filters, colors, tooltips, etc. suggested.

Here's a general overview of the notes we got:

On Step 5 - Project Characteristics page

  • remove filters by type because there's only about 7-8 projects total, could filter by tech, nature, hybrid instead
  • add tooltips to explain what each project type means

On Step 7 - Review Portfolio

  • add tooltips to explain what each project type means
  • add colors to project descriptions on the right to match to the project type colors on the left - for example, if the water project category is blue, add blue coloring to each individual water project
  • add percentage in the list of project category breakdown
  • every project is different, very diversified

On Collaborator Chat feature

  • add a button for approval

Takeaways

We really enjoyed this project and got a greater understanding of the iterative design process. It was helpful to get feedback from classmates, professors, and the CUR8 team since they each brought new insights. A purchasing flow is something we're all familiar with and seems rather straightforward, but we learned that there are actually very important considerations to make. We got to learn more about carbon removal portfolios and how you might structure a purchasing flow involving multiple stakeholders and users who might be new to the process as well. Since carbon removal portfolios are a complex product, they must be thoughtfully considered before buying, and we had to design a flow with enough friction and information so that users are satisfied with what they are purchasing.

We learned a lot from this process and are super grateful to Vitor and the CUR8 team for giving us this opportunity!
